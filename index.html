Identifying Common Pest & Termite Issues

Being aware of the signs of a pest or termite problem is the first step in addressing it effectively. Catching an infestation early can save you significant time, money, and stress. Here are some common issues and indicators that you might need professional pest control services.

Swarming Termites

One of the most visible signs of a termite infestation is the presence of swarms of winged insects inside or around your home, particularly in the spring or fall. These are reproductive termites leaving the colony to establish new ones. If you see these swarms, it’s a strong indicator of an active infestation nearby. Don’t confuse them with flying ants; termites have straight antennae, equal-sized wings, and a broad waist.

Seeing a termite swarm is a clear call to action. It means there is a mature colony present, and it’s expanding. While the swarms themselves may be temporary, they signal a much larger problem within your property or in close proximity. Ignoring swarming termites can lead to continued damage as the new colonies establish themselves.

Mud Tubes

Subterranean termites build mud tubes, also known as shelter tubes, to protect themselves as they travel from their colony in the soil to their food source (your home’s wood). These tubes are typically found on foundation walls, in crawl spaces, or along floor joists. They are made of soil and wood cellulose and are about the width of a pencil.

Finding mud tubes is a definite sign of active subterranean termite activity. These tubes serve as highways for termites, allowing them to access your home without being exposed to the open air, which can dry them out. Disturbing a mud tube and not seeing active termites doesn’t mean the problem is gone; they may just be using other tubes or foraging elsewhere. A professional inspection can confirm the presence of active termites.

Damaged Wood

Termites feed on cellulose, which is found in wood. If you notice wood in your home that sounds hollow when tapped, appears darkened or blistered, or has a maze-like pattern within it, it could be a sign of termite damage. This damage is often hidden behind walls or under floors, making it hard to detect until it’s significant.

Termite damage often starts subtly and can be easily overlooked until it’s extensive. Unlike wood rot, which is usually caused by moisture, termite damage is characterized by the tunneling and hollowing out of the wood. If you suspect termite damage, avoid probing or disturbing the area extensively, as this can cause the termites to move to another location, making treatment more difficult.

Discarded Wings

After swarming, termites shed their wings. You may find piles of these small, translucent wings near windowsills, doors, or other light sources. This is another indicator that a termite swarm has occurred, and an infestation is likely present.

Discovering discarded wings is a less direct sign of current active feeding but confirms that a termite swarm originating from your property or nearby has taken place. These wings are often found in spiderwebs or on surfaces where the termites gathered after their flight. Their presence indicates the potential for a new colony to have established itself or that a pre-existing colony is mature.

Frass (Termite Droppings)

Drywood termites, as opposed to subterranean termites, do not build mud tubes. Instead, they create small mounds of fecal pellets, known as frass, near the openings of their tunnels. This frass resembles sawdust or small grains of sand and can vary in color depending on the wood they are consuming.

Finding frass is a key indicator of drywood termite activity. Unlike subterranean termites that live in the soil, drywood termites live entirely within the wood they infest. Their frass is their way of disposing of waste from their tunnels. The presence of these small pellets, often in piles, suggests an active infestation in the nearby wood. Cleaning away the frass only temporarily removes the evidence; the termites will continue to push it out.

Bubbling or Peeling Paint

Termite activity can sometimes cause paint or wallpaper to bubble or peel, similar to moisture damage. This is because termites can introduce moisture as they tunnel through the wood or because the damage to the wood is causing the surface to deform.

While bubbling or peeling paint can be caused by many things, if it’s accompanied by other signs of termites or is in an area where termite activity is suspected, it’s worth investigating further. Professional inspection can discern whether the issue is due to moisture, termites, or another underlying problem. Termite damage can sometimes mimic other structural issues, making expert diagnosis crucial.

Visible Termites

While termites often remain hidden within wood or soil, you might occasionally see worker termites or soldiers when disturbing damaged wood or during certain times of the year. Worker termites are typically pale or translucent, while soldiers have larger, darker heads and pinchers.

Seeing live termites is a clear and undeniable sign of an infestation. Even if you only see a few, it indicates the presence of a colony. At this point, immediate professional intervention is recommended to prevent further damage. Do not attempt to treat visible termites with over-the-counter sprays, as this will likely only scatter the infestation and make it harder to effectively eliminate the entire colony.

Distant or Unusual Odors

In some cases of severe termite infestations, particularly with certain types of termites or mold growth associated with the damage, a musty or strange odor may be present near the infested area. This isn’t always a reliable indicator, but in conjunction with other signs, it can point towards a potential problem.

While less common than visual signs, an unusual odor can sometimes be a subtle clue to a hidden termite infestation. This odor might be caused by the termites themselves, the fungi they cultivate, or the decaying wood they are consuming. If you notice a persistent, unidentifiable odor, especially near wooden structures, it’s worth considering a professional inspection.

Sagging Floors or Ceilings

In advanced stages of termite infestation, the structural integrity of wooden components like floor joists and ceiling beams can be significantly compromised, leading to sagging floors or ceilings. This is a serious sign that requires immediate action.

Sagging is a late-stage symptom of extensive termite damage, indicating that the wood has been severely weakened. This level of damage can be costly to repair and poses a safety risk. Unfortunately, by the time sagging occurs, the infestation has been active for a considerable period. Professional structural assessment and repair, in addition to termite treatment, are necessary in such cases.

Termite Sounds

While not always audible, some people report hearing clicking sounds coming from within walls. This sound is often attributed to soldier termites banging their heads against the wood or shaking their bodies to warn the colony of danger when the infested wood is disturbed.

Hearing sounds from within your walls is an unsettling experience and can be a possible indicator of active pests, including termites. While professional acoustic detection equipment is more reliable for pinpointing termite activity by sound, a faint clicking or rustling can sometimes be heard in quiet environments, particularly at night. If you hear unusual sounds, an inspection is warranted.
